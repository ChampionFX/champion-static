What is Forex trading

The Foreign Exchange Market (Forex) is a global market for buying, selling, and exchanging currencies. It’s the largest market in the world where anyone can trade.

24/5 market action

Trade 24 hours a day, five days a week and take advantage of opportunities whenever they happen.

Deep liquidity

Buy and sell foreign currencies easily - even at large volumes - with minimal effect on price.

Leverage

Trade with leverage, which means the ability to control more money than the initial capital you started with.

New to Forex?

We explain fundamental concepts that give you a better understanding of how Forex works.

In a nutshell

Forex refers to the Foreign Exchange Market - the world's most liquid and most traded market, where USD 5.3 trillion worth of trades are completed each day.

Forex trading involves buying one currency and selling another currency at the same time. This is why you always see them quoted in pairs, such as EUR/USD or GBP/USD.

Which currencies are available for trading

Forex trading involves buying or selling currency pairs. When you buy a currency pair such as EUR/USD, it means that you are buying Euro and simultaneously selling the USD.

Currency pairs are categorised in the following:

  • Major pairs

    - Consist of the world's most widely traded currency pairs.

  • Minor pairs

    - Consist of less liquid currency pairs.

  • Exotic pairs

    - Consist of one major (non-USD) currency paired with the currency of an emerging economy. E.g. GBP/HKD.

What is the spread and what role does it play

When you see currency pairs offered by the broker, there are usually two prices available: the buy price and sell price.

Currency pairs are categorised in the following:

  • Major pairs

    - Consist of the world's most widely traded currency pairs.

  • Minor pairs

    - Consist of less liquid currency pairs.

  • Exotic pairs

    - Consist of one major (non-USD) currency paired with the currency of an emerging economy. E.g. GBP/HKD.

The spread is the difference between the buy (ask) price and sell (bid) price.

Buy (ask) price - sell (bid) price = spread

1.22835 - 1.22815 = 0.00020

What time is the market open for me to trade

The Forex is an over-the-counter market where trading takes place between two parties, and not with a centralised marketplace or exchange.

Trading starts when the Sydney market opens on Monday morning and ends when the New York market closes on Friday evening.

This means that you can trade Forex up to 24 hours a day, five days a week - depending on your broker.

How to trade Forex

A Forex trader has one objective in mind when trading: to exchange one currency for another in order to make a profit.

But if you’re new to Forex or financial trading of any kind, there's still a long way to go before you even purchase your first contract. This is why we've come up with a basic, step-by-step tutorial to help you bridge that gap and make your first trade.

Step 1: Learn to read currency pairs

Can’t make heads or tails of currency pairs? Here’s a simple way of looking at it:

EUR

Base currency

Also known as transaction currency

USD

Quote currency

Also known as counter currency

Tips:

  • The base currency is always equal to one unit.
  • The price of the currency pair indicates how much of the quote currency is required to buy one unit of base currency. This is more commonly known as the exchange rate.

Example:

If you see EUR/USD has a bid price of 1.05229, you’ll sell USD 1.05229 for every EUR 1 that you buy.

If the ask price is 1.05382, you'll buy USD 1.05382 for every EUR 1 that you sell.

Step 2: Understand when to buy (go long) and when to sell (go short)

You want to buy a certain currency pair if you think the base currency will go up. The reverse holds true: you want to sell that currency pair if you think the base currency will go down.

You’ve probably seen the terms ‘going long’ and ‘going short’ being used by a lot of Forex brokers and traders. Let’s compare the differences between the two terms:

Long

  • You’re buying the base currency and selling the quote currency.
  • You’re expecting the base currency to rise in value so you can sell it back for a profit.
  • Go long = buy

Short

  • You’re selling the base currency and buying the quote currency.
  • You’re expecting the base currency to fall in value so you can buy it back at a lower price (and make a profit).
  • Go short = sell

Here’s an example: how does going long or going short apply to the GBP/USD?

After your analysis, you’ve come to the conclusion that the British economy will outperform its U.S. counterpart. Thus, you decide to go long and buy into the GBP/USD, expecting the GBP to rise in value.

If you decided that the British economy will underperform instead, then you will go short on the GBP/USD.

Step 3: Buy your first currency pair

After you’ve decided which position you want to take – whether long or short – you are well on your way to buying your first currency pair.

Here’s an example of a GBP/USD currency pair and its bid-ask price.

To go long, you’ll click on ‘Buy’ to purchase GBP 1 for USD 1.22781.

To go short, you’ll click on ‘Sell’ to sell GBP 1 and receive USD 1.22771 in return.

Forex margin policy

We explain our margin policy to give you a better understanding of how Forex trading works.

What is margin?

Margin allows you to trade on leverage - giving you the same level of market exposure using much less capital.

With leverage, you can purchase 100 units of the same asset at a fraction of the cost.

Example:

If you wanted to purchase 100 units of a particular asset trading at USD 50 per unit through a traditional broker, it would cost you USD 5,000 for this transaction.

How is margin calculated?

The margin for our Forex trades is calculated as follows:

Volume (lots) x lot size / leverage = Margin

Example:

If you buy one lot of the EUR/USD symbol with a contract size of 100,000 and leverage of 100:1, the margin required to purchase that one lot will be calculated as follows:

1 x 100,000 / 100 = EUR 1,000

What’s a margin call and how do we apply it?

When your account equity is unable to cover the margin requirement, your account will be placed under margin call. This state does not affect your ability to open new positions.

What’s a stop out level and how do we apply it?

If your account is placed under margin call for an extended period of time, it will reach the stop out level where it’s unable to sustain an open position. This will lead to your pending orders being deleted, and open positions being forcibly closed (also known as forced liquidation).

Once your account hits the forced liquidation level, your orders and positions are forcibly closed in the following order:

  • We analyse your pending orders.
  • We delete an order with the largest margin reserved.
  • If your margin level is still under the stop out level, your next order will be deleted. However, orders without margin requirements will not be deleted.
  • If your margin level is still under the stop out level, we will close an open position with the largest loss.
  • We will continue to close open positions until your margin level becomes higher than the stop out level.

Important notes on our swap rates (overnight funding)

If you keep any positions open overnight, an interest adjustment will be made to your trading account as indication of the cost required to keep your position open.

This interest adjustment (or swap rate) is based on interbank lending rates, on top of a 2% fee.

The interest adjustment is calculated in points, meaning we will convert the relevant interbank lending rates to points in the base currency.

Please take note that our swap rate also depends on the time and days you hold your positions open:

  • You will be subjected to swap rates if you keep a position open past 23:59:59 GMT.
  • Positions that are still open on Wednesday at 23:59:59 GMT will be charged three times the swap rate to account for weekends - a standard practice for all Forex brokers.
  • Our swap rate may also be adjusted to take holidays into account.
